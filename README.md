## What is an LEI?

A **Legal Entity Identifier (LEI)** is a 20-character alphanumeric code used to uniquely identify legal entities involved in financial transactions. It helps create a clearer and more reliable way to identify organizations across markets, jurisdictions, and reporting systems.

An LEI is a global identifier for legal entities such as companies, funds, partnerships, trusts, and government bodies that participate in financial activity. It is designed to improve transparency and make it easier to identify the legal entity behind a transaction.

At a practical level, an LEI helps answer questions such as:

* Who is the legal entity involved in this transaction?
* Is the entity identifiable in official systems?
* Can counterparties and regulators match the entity correctly?

An LEI is not a marketing label or a brand asset. It is a standardized identifier used in financial infrastructure.

## Why LEIs matter

LEIs exist to reduce confusion in financial transactions and reporting. When organizations operate across banks, markets, and borders, a single recognized identifier helps reduce mismatches and improves trust in data.

They are commonly used in contexts such as:

* Regulatory reporting
* Securities and derivatives activity
* Cross-border transactions
* Counterparty identification
* Entity verification

For many organizations, an LEI becomes relevant when a bank, regulator, trading venue, reporting framework, or transaction process expects the legal entity to be identified in a standardized way.

## Who may need an LEI?

The exact requirement depends on jurisdiction and activity, but LEIs are commonly associated with:

* Companies
* Partnerships
* Trusts
* Funds
* Government bodies
* Non-profit organizations involved in relevant financial activity
* Other legally registered entities

A simple way to think about it: if the entity participates in regulated financial transactions or reporting, an LEI may be needed.

## Who can apply for an LEI?

LEIs are issued to **legal entities**, not to private individuals.

An entity is generally eligible if it:

* Is legally registered or constituted under national law
* Has the legal capacity to enter into contracts
* Can be identified in an official register or equivalent source
* Participates in financial transactions or regulatory reporting

A third party can also handle the application if proper authorization is in place.

## How LEI registration works

The registration process is usually straightforward when the entity details and supporting documents are ready.

A typical process includes:

1. **Create an account**
2. **Complete the online form**
3. **Upload required documents**
4. **Make payment**
5. **Review and validation**
6. **Issuance and publication**

For the official process page, see [LEI process](https://www.tnvlei.com/en-GB/process).

## What documents are commonly required?

Document requirements can vary by entity type and jurisdiction, but common requirements include:

* Certificate of incorporation or registration proof
* Letter of authority
* Board resolution or authorization letter
* National tax identification number, where applicable
* Proof of parent-subsidiary relationship, where applicable
* Power of attorney, if a third party is applying on behalf of the entity

Preparing accurate entity information in advance usually makes the process easier.

## LEI renewal

An LEI is generally valid for **one year** and needs to be renewed annually to remain active.

Renewal matters because LEI data is expected to stay current. If the renewal is missed, the LEI may no longer be considered active for uses where up-to-date entity data matters.

Renewal usually focuses on confirming the entity details and updating them if anything has changed.

## LEI transfer

If an entity already has an LEI with one accredited issuer, it may be possible to transfer the LEI to another accredited issuer.

The key point is simple:

* **The LEI number stays the same**
* The managing issuer changes

This can be useful when an entity wants to move future renewals or LEI management to a different provider.

## FAQ

### What is a Legal Entity Identifier?

A Legal Entity Identifier is a 20-character alphanumeric code that uniquely identifies a legal entity involved in financial transactions.

### Who usually requires an LEI?

LEIs are typically relevant for companies, partnerships, trusts, funds, government bodies, and other legally registered entities involved in financial transactions or regulatory reporting.

### Is an LEI mandatory?

The requirement depends on the jurisdiction and the activity involved. In many cases, financial market participants need an LEI for regulated financial activity or reporting.

### Can an individual apply for an LEI?

No. LEIs are issued to legal entities, not to private individuals.

### Who is eligible for an LEI?

A legal entity is generally eligible if it is legally established, can be identified in an official register, has the legal capacity to enter contracts, and participates in financial transactions or reporting.

### How long is an LEI valid?

An LEI is generally valid for one year and needs annual renewal to remain active.

### Can an existing LEI be transferred?

Yes. An LEI can usually be transferred to another accredited issuer, and the LEI number itself remains unchanged.

### What happens if the entity already has an LEI with another provider?

The entity may be able to transfer that LEI instead of applying for a new one.

### Can a consultant or third party apply on behalf of an entity?

Yes. A third party can usually apply if a valid authorization document, such as a letter of authority or power of attorney, is provided.

### What documents are commonly needed for registration?

Common requirements include official registration documents, authorization documents, and supporting entity information. Additional documents may be needed depending on the entity structure and jurisdiction.
